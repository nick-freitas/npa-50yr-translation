The 50-Year Trajectory of the Japanese Police and New Developments

Fifty years have passed since the current Police Law was enacted.

The current police system, born in 1954 (Showa 29), was constructed upon reflection on and examination of the former system, and is a unique system without parallel elsewhere. Over the subsequent 50 years, the police have confronted numerous difficult public safety challenges and responded appropriately to various demands for administrative reform. While partial institutional revisions have been made along the way, the basic framework of the police system has been firmly maintained and has taken root as a system well-suited to our country. This is largely due to the fact that democratic management of the police by Public Safety Commissions functions properly, that the system is fundamentally based on autonomous local police with an appropriate division of roles between the national and local governments, and that political neutrality is ensured. The 50-year history demonstrates that the two objectives of the Police Law -- "to guarantee the management and operation of the police based on democratic principles, and to establish a police organization sufficient to carry out its duties efficiently" -- have been steadily achieved.

Accordingly, we have taken this opportunity to reaffirm the path that the Japanese police have traveled. Section 1 organizes and introduces the history of the birth and revisions of the current police system. Section 2 reviews and examines, roughly by decade, the public safety challenges that the postwar Japanese police have faced and the countermeasures taken. Building on these, Section 3 examines the future direction and ideal form of police administration going forward.

In order to further strengthen the police system and deal with the serious public safety situation, it remains important to listen sincerely to the demands of the times, to supplement areas that are lacking, and to constantly verify the effectiveness of the basic framework of the system. This special feature represents the first step in such an endeavor.

Section 1: The Birth and Evolution of the Current Police System

1. The Enactment of the Old Police Law and the Democratization of the Police

(1) The Prewar Police System

The prewar police system was modeled after the legal systems of continental law countries following the Meiji Restoration, and was fundamentally a national police system. The Minister of Home Affairs, as the responsible minister, directed and supervised the Metropolitan Police Superintendent-General and prefectural governors serving as regional administrators. These regional administrators in turn directed and supervised the Metropolitan Police Department and the prefectural police departments (police bureaus) and the police stations placed under them, all functioning as national agencies. The scope of duties handled by police agencies was broader than today, and though it varied by era, they also had jurisdiction over matters related to public health, construction, labor, and other affairs.

(2) The Enactment of the Old Police Law

Immediately after the end of the war in August 1945 (Showa 20), the General Headquarters of the Allied Forces (GHQ), which was promoting the democratization of Japan, sought to carry out a fundamental reform of the police system. However, given the chaotic social conditions, it was decided that the police system would be maintained as it was for the time being, even as the constitution and local government systems were being revised. Subsequently, after coordination of opinions within GHQ, a final GHQ draft was prepared based on a ruling by General MacArthur made at the request of Prime Minister Katayama, leading to the enactment of the Old Police Law (Law No. 196 of December 17, 1947 / Showa 22).

Principles and Features of the Old Police Law

- Decentralization of the Police

The former national police system was reformed, and the system was fundamentally based on municipal autonomous police forces. All cities and urban towns and villages with a population of 5,000 or more maintained their own police forces, while other areas (mainly rural villages) fell under the jurisdiction of the National Rural Police, a national agency. Municipal police forces were not subject to the direction or supervision of the National Rural Police except in cases of national emergency. The prefectural National Rural Police were managed and operated by Prefectural Public Safety Commissions placed under the jurisdiction of the governor.

- Democratic Management of the Police

The Public Safety Commission system was adopted as a mechanism to manage the police democratically and ensure its political neutrality. Public Safety Commissions were collegial bodies composed of commissioners who were representatives of the citizenry, established at the national, prefectural, and municipal levels, exercising their authority independently of the Prime Minister, prefectural governors, and municipal mayors.

- Limitation of Police Responsibilities

The responsibilities of the police were defined as protecting the lives, persons, and property of the people, investigating crimes, arresting suspects, and maintaining public safety. It was made clear that police activities should be strictly limited to the scope of these responsibilities, and provisions were specifically included to caution against the abuse of police powers.

第1節　現行警察制度の誕生と変遷

1　旧警察法の制定と警察の民主化

（1）戦前の警察制度

戦前の警察制度は、明治維新後に大陸法系諸国の法制度に倣って作られたもので、国家警察を基本としていた。内務大臣が主任の大臣として、地方長官たる警視総監及び府県知事等を指揮監督し、これらの地方長官は、国の機関としての警視庁及び道府県（警察部）とその下に置かれた警察署等を指揮監督した。また、警察機関の業務の範囲は、現在より広く、時代により違いはあるが、衛生、建築、労働等に関する事務も所掌していた。

（2）旧警察法の制定

昭和20年8月の終戦直後、日本の民主化を進める連合国軍総司令部は、警察制度の根本的改革を図ろうとしたが、混乱した社会情勢等を踏まえ、憲法及び地方制度の改正に当たっても、当分の間、警察制度は現状を維持することとした。その後、総司令部内における意見の調整を経た後、片山内閣総理大臣の求めによりなされたマッカーサー元帥の裁定により、総司令部の最終案が作成され、旧警察法（昭和22年12月17日法律第196号）の制定に至った。

旧警察法の理念と特徴

■　警察の地方分権

それまでの国家警察制度を改め、市町村の自治体警察を基本とした。すべての市及び人口5,000人以上の市街的町村は自ら警察を維持する一方、その他の地域（主として村落部）は国の機関である国家地方警察の管轄とした。市町村警察は、国家非常事態の場合を除いて、国家地方警察の指揮監督を受けず、また、都道府県の国家地方警察は、知事の所轄の下に置かれる都道府県公安委員会がその運営を管理した。

■　警察の民主的管理

警察を民主的に管理し、かつ、その政治的中立性を確保する制度として、公安委員会制度を採り入れた。公安委員会は、市民の代表者たる委員によって構成される合議体の機関であり、国、都道府県及び市町村に置かれ、内閣総理大臣、都道府県知事及び市町村長から独立して職権を行使した。

■　警察の責務の限定

警察の責務を、国民の生命、身体及び財産の保護に任じ、犯罪の捜査、被疑者の逮捕及び公安の維持に当たることとし、警察の活動は厳格にその責務の範囲内に限られるべきことを明らかにした上、権能の濫用を戒める旨を特に規定した。

(3) Problems with the Old Police Law

The Old Police Law was of epoch-making significance as a measure to democratize the police; however, because the national circumstances of Japan were not fully taken into account, numerous problems arose from the very beginning of its implementation.

1. Problems Caused by the Fragmentation of Police Organizations

As a result of adopting the municipal police system, as many as 1,605 autonomous police forces were established, fragmenting the units of police activity. This made it difficult to respond efficiently and accurately to group-based or wide-area crimes.

2. Problems Concerning Expenses

The costs of autonomous police forces became a heavy fiscal burden, particularly for small-scale municipalities. On the other hand, in towns and villages with populations under 5,000, which fell under the jurisdiction of the National Rural Police, police expenses were borne entirely by the national government, creating an imbalance. Moreover, the dual structure of the police apparatus and the fragmentation of organizations led to organizational complexity, duplication of facilities and personnel, and an uneconomical system that imposed a heavy fiscal burden on the public.

3. Problems Concerning the Nature of Police Affairs

Despite the fact that police affairs possess both national and local characteristics, they were divided among organizations biased toward either a national or local character based on mere geographic divisions. Furthermore, the national government had no involvement at all in the Metropolitan Police Department, which handled affairs of a strongly national character as the capital's police force, and its expenses were borne by the municipality. Meanwhile, rural police forces handling affairs of a weakly national character were maintained by the national budget and were beyond the oversight of local government assemblies -- a contradictory arrangement.

4. Problems with the Public Safety Commission System and the Government's Responsibility for Public Safety

The Public Safety Commission system was highly valued as a new method of police management; however, conversely, the National Public Safety Commission had strong independence from the Cabinet. As a result, the Cabinet could not adequately fulfill its political responsibility regarding public safety, and questions were raised that this ran counter to the requirements of democracy from this perspective.

（3）旧警察法の問題点

旧警察法は、警察の民主化を図るものとして画期的な意義を有するものであったが、我が国の国情が十分に加味されていない面もあって、施行当初から多くの問題が生じた。

①　警察組織の細分化による問題

市町村警察制度を採り入れた結果、1,605にも上る自治体警察が置かれ、警察活動の単位が細分化された。このため、集団的又は広域的な犯罪等に対して、効率的かつ的確な対応をすることが困難となった。

②　経費をめぐる問題

自治体警察の経費は、特に小規模の自治体にとって重い財政負担となった。その一方、国家地方警察の管轄とされた人口5,000人未満の町村では警察経費が全額国費負担となり、均衡も失していた。また、警察機構を二本立てとし、組織も細分化したことで、組織の複雑化と施設、人員等の重複が生じ、不経済で国民に大きな財政負担を強いるものとなった。

③　事務の性格に関する問題

警察事務は、国家的性格と地方的性格を併有するものであるにもかかわらず、単なる地理的な区分によって、国又は地方のいずれかの性格に偏った組織により分担されることとなった。しかも、それは、首都警察として国家的性格の強い事務を処理する警視庁に対しても、国の関与は全くなく、経費も自治体の負担とされる一方で、国家的性格の弱い事務を処理する村落部の警察が、国の予算によって維持され、地方公共団体の議会の監督が及ばないなど矛盾のある分担であった。

④　公安委員会制度と政府の治安責任の問題

公安委員会制度は、新しい警察管理方式として高く評価されたが、反面、国家公安委員会の内閣に対する独立性が強かった。そのため、治安に関して内閣が政治責任を十分に果たすことができず、こうした観点からの民主主義の要請に反するとの疑問が呈された。

The Old Police Law was amended eight times over seven years following its implementation in an effort to remedy its drawbacks. However, the fundamental problems inherent in the system could not be corrected, and many municipalities that could not bear the fiscal burden relinquished their autonomous police forces. (At the time of the Old Police Law's implementation, there were 1,605 autonomous police forces, of which 1,386 were town and village police forces; but just before the enactment of the current Police Law, the number of autonomous police forces had decreased to 402, of which only 127 were town and village police forces.)

As it happened, public safety in the regions had deteriorated to an extreme degree; violent crimes such as murder and robbery were occurring with increasing frequency, and large-scale group violence incidents rooted in social unrest were breaking out one after another. The reexamination of the police system and structure thus became a matter of grave public concern. When Japan regained its independence in April 1952 (Showa 27), the fundamental reform of the police system was rapidly given concrete form as part of the correction of Occupation-era policies.

Thus, the government submitted a police law bill to the 15th session of the National Diet in February 1953 (Showa 28), seeking to build a new system to replace the Old Police Law. This bill, like the current Police Law, aimed to make the prefecture the basic unit of policing and to prescribe national government involvement in light of the national character of police affairs; however, it was scrapped due to the dissolution of the House of Representatives.

Thereafter, police system reform was also debated in the Local System Research Council (established in 1952 / Showa 27). In its report of October 1953 (Showa 28), the Council expressed the following views: (1) the basic unit of policing should be the prefecture, with city police forces established as exceptions in major cities; and (2) the national government should direct and supervise prefectural police in relation to cases of national importance and should bear a certain share of police expenses.

Based on this report and the deliberation process of the 15th Diet session, the government proceeded with the examination of a new system. Principles and Features of the New Police Law

- Guaranteeing Democratic Operation of the Police

As with the Old Police Law, the Public Safety Commission system was retained at both the national and local levels to guarantee the democratic operation of the police. Additionally, by delegating police affairs to the prefectures as an entrusted function, the prefectural police were made subject to oversight by residents through the prefectural assemblies.

- Efficient Operation of Police Organizations (Decentralization and Unification at the Prefectural Level with Limited National Involvement)

In order to make the scale of police organizations rational and efficient for dealing with actual police incidents, the prefecture was made the constituent unit, and police affairs in major cities were also to be handled in a unified manner by the prefectural police. On the other hand, although police affairs possess both national and local characteristics, it is difficult to clearly distinguish on a case-by-case basis whether any particular function is national or local in nature. Therefore, while unifying the police organizations that carry out enforcement operations under the prefectural police, systems were established -- including the Commissioner General of the National Police Agency's authority to direct and supervise, the national treasury disbursement system for local police budgets, and the local police officer system whereby senior officials are national government employees -- to allow the national government to be involved in local police operations within a defined scope.

- Ensuring Political Neutrality and Cabinet Responsibility for Public Safety

Since the Cabinet bears responsibility to the Diet for police administration as well and holds the right to submit bills to the Diet, the Chairperson of the National Public Safety Commission was to be filled by a Minister of State. Furthermore, the appointment and dismissal of the Commissioner General of the National Police Agency and the Superintendent General of the Metropolitan Police Department were made subject to the approval of the Prime Minister, thereby clarifying the Cabinet's responsibility for public safety.

新警察法の理念と特徴

■　警察の民主的運営の保障

旧警察法と同じく、公安委員会制度を国と地方の双方で存続させ、警察の民主的な運営の保障を図るとともに、都道府県に警察事務を団体委任することで、都道府県議会を通じた住民の監視を受けることとした。

■　警察組織の能率的運営（都道府県への分権・一元化と国の限定的関与）

警察組織の規模を、現実に発生する警察事象の処理に当たる上で合理的かつ効率的なものとするため、都道府県を構成単位とし、大都市の警察事務も都道府県警察で一元的に処理することとした。他方、警察事務は国家的性格と地方的性格を併有しているものの、個々の事務ごとにその性格が国家的であるか地方的であるかを明確に区別することが困難であることから、執行事務を行う警察組織を都道府県警察に一元化しつつ、警察庁長官の指揮監督制度、地方の警察予算の国庫支弁制度、上級幹部職員を国の職員とする地方警務官制度等を設け、一定の範囲で地方の警察運営に国が関与することとした。

Section 2: 50-Year History of Japanese Police

1. Japanese Police in the Showa 20s (1945-1954)

The Showa 20s was an era in which the economy recovered to prewar levels after passing through the postwar period of turmoil. Following acceptance of the Potsdam Declaration in August of Showa 20 (1945), democratization policies were advanced by the Supreme Commander for the Allied Powers (SCAP), and the new Constitution was promulgated in Showa 21 (1946). The National Police Reserve was established in Showa 24 (1949), the Korean War broke out in June of Showa 25 (1950), and the Korean War procurement boom promoted Japan's recovery. In Showa 26 (1951), the Treaty of Peace with Japan and the Japan-U.S. Security Treaty were concluded, and Japan returned to the international community. In Showa 28 (1953), regular television broadcasting began, and the public became enthralled by live professional wrestling broadcasts.

Amid these developments, crime, which had decreased during the war, surged sharply, reflecting the material deprivation and decline in moral consciousness brought about by defeat, and in Showa 23 (1948) and Showa 24 (1949), the number of known Penal Code offenses reached nearly 1.6 million. Entering the latter half of the Showa 20s, property crimes decreased due to economic stabilization, but violent crimes remained at high levels, and homicides peaked at 3,081 cases in Showa 29 (1954).

(1) Enactment of the Code of Criminal Procedure and Establishment of Primary Investigation Authority

The Showa 20s was the era in which the foundations of today's criminal investigation police were laid. Before the war, police officers were considered to conduct criminal investigations in a supplementary capacity under the direction of prosecutors; however, Article 189, Paragraph 2 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, which came into effect in Showa 24 (1949), stipulated that "when judicial police officials consider that a crime has been committed, they shall investigate the offender and evidence," and the police came to conduct investigations as an independent primary investigative agency.

Accompanying this, the need arose for the police to establish, by their own hand, general rules regarding criminal investigation, and in Showa 24 (1949), the Criminal Investigation Regulations were enacted as a directive of the Commissioner General of the National Rural Police. Then, in Showa 25 (1950), Criminal Investigation Regulations with further enriched content were enacted as National Public Safety Commission Rules.

Subsequently, criticism was also directed at the investigative practices of the newly established police. In particular, regarding the abuse of arrest authority, the Showa 28 (1953) amendment to the Code of Criminal Procedure restricted the authority to request arrest warrants to judicial police officers of the rank of police inspector or above designated by the Public Safety Commission. Taking this as an opportunity, the police sought to innovate and improve the conduct of investigations and enacted the current Criminal Investigation Regulations in Showa 32 (1957).

（1）刑事訴訟法の制定と第一次捜査権の確立

20年代は、今日の刑事警察の基礎が築かれた時代であった。戦前、警察官は、検察官の指揮の下に、その補助的な立場で犯罪捜査を行うものとされていたが、24年に施行された刑事訴訟法第189条第2項で「司法警察職員は、犯罪があると思料するときは、犯人及び証拠を捜査するものとする。」と規定され、警察は独立した第一次捜査機関として捜査を行うこととなった。

これに伴い、警察自らの手によって犯罪捜査に関する一般準則を定める必要が生じ、24年に国家地方警察本部長訓令として犯罪捜査規範が制定された。そして、25年には、その内容を更に充実させた犯罪捜査規範が国家公安委員会規則として制定された。

(2) Expansion of the Japanese Communist Party's Influence and Development of Violent Destructive Activities

The Japanese Communist Party (JCP), which was legalized in Showa 20 (1945), expanded its party strength against the backdrop of the impoverishment of the people's livelihood and social unrest following defeat in the war, while also devoting efforts to organizing labor unions and mass organizations and rapidly expanding its influence. In the 22nd House of Representatives general election in Showa 21 (1946), the JCP received approximately 2.14 million votes and won 5 seats--the party's first seats since its founding. In the 24th House of Representatives general election in Showa 24 (1949), it received approximately 2.98 million votes and won 35 seats.

Subsequently, at the 5th National Conference in Showa 26 (1951), the JCP adopted the "1951 Platform," which stated that "it is a mistake to think that Japan's liberation and democratic transformation can be achieved by peaceful means," and carried out violent destructive activities including the shooting death of Inspector Shiratori (Showa 27/1952) and the Osu Riot Incident (Showa 27/1952).

To deal with this, in Showa 27 (1952), the police established riot police units in 20 prefectures and worked toward the early suppression and arrest of those engaged in collective illegal acts. Furthermore, the public severely criticized the JCP for its repeated violent destructive activities, and in the 25th House of Representatives general election in Showa 27 (1952), all officially endorsed candidates lost their seats, and the party's strength declined dramatically.

(3) Establishment of Patrol Police and Enactment of Security Police-Related Legislation

After the end of the war, the increase in vagrants and runaways became a social problem, but with the enactment of the Child Welfare Act in Showa 22 (1947) and the Public Assistance Act in Showa 25 (1950), the police actively cooperated with the relief activities of prefectures and other entities and addressed these issues. Also during the same period, the 110 emergency reporting system was put into operation (Showa 23/1948), field duty service regulations were established, and patrol cars were deployed (Showa 25/1950), establishing a patrol police system that used police boxes (koban) and residential police boxes (chuzaisho) as bases to conduct patrols and regular liaison visits and to respond promptly to incidents and accidents.

The deterioration of public morals gave rise to numerous security police-related laws and regulations. The Amusement Business Control Act (Showa 23/1948), newly enacted following the abolition of prewar regulations, extended regulatory coverage to cafes, cabarets, dance halls, and the like. Street prostitution, which had surged dramatically amid the postwar turmoil, was regulated through local ordinances, and the Anti-Prostitution Act was enacted in Showa 31 (1956). Regarding secondhand dealers and pawnbrokers, to prevent the circulation of stolen goods and facilitate their discovery, the Secondhand Goods Business Act was enacted in Showa 24 (1949) and the Pawnbroking Business Act in Showa 25 (1950). Regarding firearms regulation, the Firearms Possession Prohibition Order was enacted in Showa 21 (1946), the Firearms and Swords Possession Control Order in Showa 25 (1950), and the Firearms and Swords Possession Control Act in Showa 33 (1958), with possession prohibited in principle. Furthermore, as large quantities of stimulant drugs (Philopon/methamphetamine) flooded onto the market and spread rapidly through a society devastated by defeat, the Stimulant Drugs Control Act prohibiting the possession and use of stimulant drugs was enacted in Showa 26 (1951), and the police strengthened their crackdown on stimulant drug offenses.

（2）日本共産党の勢力拡大と暴力的破壊活動の展開

20年に合法化された日本共産党は、敗戦後の国民生活の窮乏と社会不安を背景に党勢を増大させるとともに、労働組合や大衆団体の組織化に力を入れ、急速に影響力を拡大した。21年の第22回衆議院議員総選挙では、約214万票を得て結党以来初の議席となる5議席を獲得し、24年の第24回衆議院議員総選挙では、約298万票を得て35議席を獲得した。

その後、26年の第5回全国協議会で「日本の解放と民主的変革を、平和の手段によって達成しうると考えるのはまちがいである」とする「51年綱領」を採択し、白鳥警部射殺事件（27年）、大須騒擾事件（27年）等の暴力的破壊活動を展開した。

警察は、これに対処するため、27年、20都道府県に機動隊を創設し、集団的違法行為の早期鎮圧と検挙活動に努めた。また、暴力的破壊活動を繰り返す日本共産党を国民は厳しく批判し、27年の第25回衆議院議員総選挙では公認候補が全員落選するなど、党勢は大幅に衰退した。

（3）外勤警察の確立と保安警察関係法令の制定

終戦後、浮浪者や家出人の増加が社会問題となったが、22年に児童福祉法が、25年に生活保護法が制定され、警察は、都道府県等の救護活動に積極的に協力し、その対策に当たった。また、同時期に、110番通報制度の運用（23年）、外勤勤務準則の制定、パトカーの配備（25年）を行い、派出所・駐在所を拠点として警らや巡回連絡を行い事件・事故に即応する外勤警察を確立した。

2. Japanese Police in the Showa 30s (1955-1964)

The Showa 30s was an era that achieved high economic growth, beginning with the Jimmu Boom. In Showa 31 (1956), diplomatic relations with the Soviet Union were restored, and Japan's admission to the United Nations was approved. In Showa 34 (1959), His Imperial Highness the Crown Prince (at that time) was married, and the nation was buoyed by the Iwato Boom; however, the negotiations over revision of the Japan-U.S. Security Treaty, which began in earnest that same year, led to the intensification of the Anpo (Security Treaty) struggle the following year. In Showa 39 (1964), Japan joined the OECD and entered the ranks of advanced capitalist nations; moreover, the Tokaido Shinkansen opened and the first Olympic Games in Asia were held in Tokyo, impressing upon both domestic and international audiences the nation's recovery.

On the other hand, the high crime rates seen during the turmoil immediately after the war subsided, and Penal Code offenses leveled off at around 1.4 million cases per year. Within this, what increased significantly were violent crimes and rape, which reached their postwar peak in the Showa 30s. It was also an era in which the advancement of motorization accompanying economic growth and the expansion of individuals' living spheres were vividly reflected in crime trends.

（1）自動車交通の発展と道路交通法の制定

戦後の経済成長に伴う自動車の急速な普及を背景に、交通事故死者数は20年代後半から著しい増加傾向を示した。これに対応するため、35年に道路交通取締法が廃止され、新たに道路交通法が制定された。この法律は、取締りの根拠法にとどまらず、すべての者が安全に道路を通行するために遵守すべき道路交通の基本法たる性格を有するものである。その後、同法は、高速道路における自動車の交通方法の特例の新設（38年）、道路交通に関する条約への加入に伴う国際運転免許証等に係る制度の導入（39年）等の改正が行われ、道路標識、区画線及び道路標示に関する命令（35年）、自動車の保管場所の確保等に関する法律（37年）等の関連法令も順次整備された。

(2) Institutional Reforms for Dealing with the Widening Geographic Scope of Crime

The development of automobile transportation also brought about the geographic expansion of crime. In order to strengthen wide-area investigations, police fundamentally revised the existing criminal modus operandi system (a system for organizationally collecting, managing, and utilizing materials related to criminal methods) and established the Rules for Handling Criminal Modus Operandi Materials (Showa 31 [1956]), as well as newly establishing the Rules for Mutual Assistance in Criminal Investigation, which set the basic framework for mutual cooperation among prefectural police forces (Showa 32 [1957]). Subsequently, triggered by the occurrence of serial robbery-murders spanning wide areas (the Nishiguchi Case, Showa 38-39 [1963-1964]), the Special Investigation Guidelines for Wide-Area Major Cases were established, introducing a system whereby wide-area major cases designated by the National Police Agency would be investigated by the relevant prefectural police forces under national coordination.

Additionally, in the Showa 38 (1963) kidnapping and murder of an infant in Iriya, Tokyo (the Yoshinobu-chan Case), and the kidnapping and murder of a high school girl for ransom in Sayama City, Saitama Prefecture (the Sayama Case), police had opportunities to make contact with and arrest the perpetrators but allowed them to escape, and the victims were also killed, leading to severe criticism of the police. For this reason, the police formulated the Criminal Police Strengthening Countermeasures Guidelines in the same year, and undertook to enhance and strengthen criminal policing through strengthening the education of investigators, improving the investigation system, and other measures.

(3) The 1960 Anpo Struggle and the Rise of Mass and Labor Movements

In Showa 34 (1959), as negotiations for revising the Japan-U.S. Security Treaty began in earnest, the National Council for Blocking Revision of the Security Treaty was formed by the Japan Socialist Party, the General Council of Trade Unions of Japan (Sohyo), and others, and nationwide unified actions opposing the treaty revision were repeatedly carried out (the 1960 Anpo Struggle). In the course of this, the All-Japan Federation of Student Self-Government Associations (Zengakuren), led by far-left violent groups, carried out radical actions including the Diet Building Intrusion Incident (Showa 34 [1959]) and the Haneda Airport Lobby Occupation Incident (Showa 35 [1960]).

Additionally, accompanying changes in industrial structure and social conditions, the labor movement also became more active. In the Mitsui Miike Dispute (Showa 35 [1960]), which originated from the designated dismissal of coal mine union members, strikes and other struggles were carried out with support from Sohyo and others, giving rise to mass brawling incidents.

Meanwhile, among right-wing groups that felt a sense of crisis at the intensification of left-wing movements, the assassination of Japan Socialist Party Chairman Asanuma in Showa 35 (1960) served as a catalyst for the open advocacy of theories of ethnic self-defense and rationalization of coups d'etat, which held that the use of force was unavoidable to save the nation and people from crisis. In Showa 36 (1961), the Three Nothings Incident (Sanmu Jiken), described as the first attempted coup d'etat in the postwar period, was uncovered.

（2）広域化する犯罪に対処するための制度改正

自動車交通の発展は、犯罪の広域化ももたらした。警察は、広域捜査を強化するため、従来の犯罪手口制度（犯罪手口に関する資料を組織的に収集・管理・運用する制度）を抜本的に見直して犯罪手口資料取扱規則を制定するとともに（31年）、都道府県警察相互の協力関係の基本を定めた犯罪捜査共助規則を新たに制定した（32年）。その後、広域にわたる連続強盗殺人事件（西口事件、38、39年）の発生を契機に広域重要事件特別捜査要綱を制定し、警察庁が指定した広域重要事件は、国の調整の下に関係都道府県警察が捜査を行うという制度を導入した。

また、38年の東京都入谷の幼児誘拐殺人事件（吉展ちゃん事件）や埼玉県狭山市の身の代金目的の女子高校生誘拐殺人事件（狭山事件）では、犯人と接触し逮捕する機会がありながらこれを取り逃がし、被害者も殺害されたことから、警察に対して厳しい批判がなされた。このため、警察は、同年に刑事警察強化対策要綱を策定し、捜査員の教育の強化、捜査体制の整備等により刑事警察の充実強化を図ることとした。

3. Japanese Police in the Showa 40s (1965-1974)

The Showa 40s was an era that recorded the second phase of rapid economic growth and unprecedented economic expansion. (1) The 1970 Anpo Struggle and the Radicalization of Far-Left Violent Groups

Far-left violent groups, against the backdrop of rising anti-war and anti-American sentiment sparked by the Vietnam War and the anti-establishment mood born from campus disputes, set their sights on the revision of the Japan-U.S. Security Treaty in Showa 45 (1970) and waged a radical 1970 Anpo struggle. Combined with campus disputes, the Narita struggle, and other conflicts, these drew in students and workers dissatisfied with the political and social status quo, growing into large-scale movements.

After the Japan-U.S. Security Treaty was automatically renewed in Showa 45 (1970), their activities gradually became more radical. Armed struggle escalated without limit -- from the bombing murder at the residence of the Metropolitan Police Department Personnel Division Chief (Showa 46 [1971]), to the Asama Mountain Lodge Incident and the Tel Aviv Lod Airport Incident (Showa 47 [1972]), to the Mitsubishi Heavy Industries Building Bombing (Showa 49 [1974]). Internal factional conflicts resulting in numerous deaths were also repeated. However, due to their extremism, they rapidly lost the support of the students and workers who had previously sympathized with them, and far-left violent groups became socially isolated.

Amid this, in the Showa 49 (1974) Spring Offensive, wave-like struggles were waged mainly by public sector workers under slogans such as "Major Wage Increases" and "Recovery of the Right to Strike." Illegal strikes by public workers, such as the Japan Teachers' Union April 11 Local Public Service Law Violation Incident, and labor incidents involving organizational conflicts within labor unions, occurred frequently.

On the other hand, right-wing groups were stimulated by the heightening of the Anpo struggle and the Japan Communist Party's Democratic Coalition Government concept of Showa 45 (1970), leading to incidents such as the attack on JCP Chairman Miyamoto (Showa 48 [1973]). Additionally, the Mishima Incident (Showa 45 [1970]) served as a catalyst for the birth of the "New Right," which advocated anti-American and anti-establishment positions.

(2) Strengthening Efforts Against the Increase in Traffic Accident Deaths

The number of traffic accident fatalities, which had shown a marked increasing trend since the latter half of the Showa 20s (late 1940s-early 1950s), reached 16,765 in Showa 45 (1970), and this grave situation was called the "Traffic War" (First). Accordingly, in Showa 41 (1966), the Emergency Measures Law Concerning Traffic Safety Facility Improvement Projects was enacted, and under the long-term plan based on this law, traffic safety facilities such as traffic signals and road signs were urgently developed. Furthermore, in Showa 45 (1970), the Basic Act on Traffic Safety Measures was enacted, establishing a framework in which national and local government agencies worked together with the public and related organizations to address traffic safety measures.

With regard to laws under police jurisdiction, the Road Traffic Act and related ordinances were amended repeatedly, introducing the Safe Driving Manager system (Showa 40 [1965]), the Traffic Violation Notification system (Showa 43 [1968]), the points system for administrative disposition of driver's licenses (Showa 44 [1969]), and the on-road testing system for ordinary driver's licenses (Showa 48 [1973]), among others. Additionally, the establishment of traffic patrol officers (Showa 45 [1970]) and the increase in traffic police officers (from Showa 47 [1972] onward) strengthened the organizational framework.

As a result of these efforts, from Showa 45 (1970) onward, the number of fatalities, injuries, and traffic accidents all turned to decline, and by Showa 54 (1979), the number of fatalities had fallen to 8,466 -- roughly halved compared to the peak.

(3) Responding to Social Problems Accompanying Urbanization

While rapid economic growth greatly improved the living standards of the populace, the mass-production, mass-consumption economy gave rise to various pollution problems. Following the so-called "Pollution Diet Session" of Showa 45 (1970), pollution-related laws and regulations were progressively developed. The police also strengthened enforcement against water pollution from factory effluent, mass dumping of malodorous substances, and other violations, and responded to the rapidly increasing number of pollution-related complaints. In Showa 49 (1974), a Pollution Section was established within the NPA's Security Department to strengthen the organizational framework. Regarding air pollution, noise, and vibration caused by automobiles, the Road Traffic Act was amended in Showa 45 (1970) to add "contributing to the prevention of hazards arising from road traffic" to the law's objectives, and provisions for preventing traffic-related pollution were developed.

Additionally, reflecting the rise of hedonistic trends amid the economic boom, businesses such as bathhouses with private rooms and strip theaters increased, and furthermore, as a byproduct of the spread of private automobile ownership, motel businesses proliferated rapidly around trunk roads. To prevent the harmful effects of these establishments, bathhouses with private rooms and strip theaters were made subject to regulation under the Entertainment and Amusement Trades Control Act in Showa 41 (1966), and motel businesses were added in Showa 47 (1972).

In addition, in Showa 47 (1972), the Security Guard Business Act was enacted to prevent recurring improper conduct by security guards in the security industry, which had rapidly developed against the backdrop of corporate rationalization and labor shortages.

（2）交通事故死者数増加に対する取組みの強化

20年代後半から著しい増加傾向にあった交通事故死者数は、45年には16,765人に達し、その深刻な状況は交通戦争（第1次）と呼ばれた。そこで、41年に交通安全施設等整備事業に関する緊急措置法が制定され、同法に基づく長期計画の下、信号機、道路標識等の交通安全施設等が緊急に整備されることとなった。また、45年に交通安全対策基本法が制定され、国及び地方の行政機関と国民や関係団体が一体となって交通安全対策に取り組む体制が構築された。

このような取組みの結果、45年を境に死者数、負傷者数、交通事故発生件数のいずれも減少に転じ、54年には死者数が8,466人となり、ピーク時に比べてほぼ半減した。

（3）都市化の進展に伴う社会問題への対応

高度経済成長により国民の生活水準は大きく向上したが、大量生産・大量消費型の経済は、様々な公害問題を引き起こした。45年のいわゆる公害国会以降、公害関係法令が順次整備されたが、警察でも、工場廃液による水質汚濁や悪臭原因物の大量投棄等の取締りを強化し、急増する公害関係の苦情に対応するとともに、49年には警察庁保安部に公害課を設置して体制を強化した。また、自動車による大気汚染、騒音及び振動に対しては、45年に道路交通法が改正され、法の目的に「道路の交通に起因する障害の防止に資すること」が加わり、交通公害の防止を図るための規定が整備された。

(2) Qualitative Changes in Crime and the Promotion of White-Collar Crime Investigation and Scientific Investigation

As the economy entered a period of stable growth and society became more mature and complex, forms of crime qualitatively different from those of the past emerged. Reflecting the massive growth of the consumer society and economic recession, fraud cases exploiting insurance systems and credit cards, consumer troubles involving consumer finance and door-to-door sales, and similar incidents occurred frequently. (3) Promotion of Crime Victim Support Measures

In the Mitsubishi Heavy Industries Building bombing that occurred in Showa 49 (1974) (8 dead, 330 injured), numerous completely innocent persons including company employees and nearby pedestrians were killed or injured. Even among these victims, a striking imbalance arose between those who received public benefits under the Workers' Accident Compensation Insurance Act and those who received no compensation whatsoever. For this reason, the Crime Victims Benefits Payment Act was enacted in Showa 55 (1980), under which the state would provide benefits to the bereaved families of those who suffered unexpected death through criminal acts or to those left with disabilities. This law was Japan's first legislation aimed at providing relief to crime victims. Following amendments including expansion of eligibility to cover serious illness and injury (Heisei 13 [2001]), over the 23 years through the end of Heisei 15 (2003), approximately 14.3 billion yen in benefits were paid to 6,132 persons, and the law has continued to play an important role in mitigating the harm of criminal victimization up to the present day.

Additionally, as symposia and surveys on the actual conditions of crime victims (Heisei 3-4 [1991-1992]) highlighted the importance of reducing psychological harm, the NPA formulated the "Victim Support Guidelines" (Heisei 8 [1996]) and advanced the development of a system for notifying victims of the disposition status of suspects, the establishment of consultation and counseling services for victims, and other measures. Together with the newly established Crime Victims Support Office at the NPA (Heisei 8 [1996]) and the establishment of the Victim Support Network (Heisei 10 [1998]), the organizational framework for promoting victim support measures has been strengthened.

(1) Enactment of the Anti-Organized Crime Law in Response to Growing Public Demand

In the postwar period, in addition to traditional violent groups such as bakuto (gamblers) and tekiya (street vendors), a new type of violent group called gurentai (street gangs) emerged. The police consistently prioritized crackdowns on organized crime in the postwar period, carrying out targeted enforcement against core leadership (the "summit strategy") and other measures, achieving significant results such as major reductions in membership. However, organized crime groups further diversified and refined their activities, generating numerous cases of civil intervention by violence. Beginning with the conflict between the Fourth-Generation Yamaguchi-gumi and the Ichiwa-kai (from Showa 60 / 1985 onward), they engaged in repeated turf wars that killed and injured numerous bystander citizens and police officers, causing public opinion demanding stronger organized crime countermeasures to surge dramatically.

Through cease-and-desist orders against violent demands and other measures, the conduct of organized crime members became subject to regulation over a much broader scope. (2) Police Response to Advancing Internationalization

As the internationalization of society progressed, illegal employment cases increased against the backdrop of wage disparities with neighboring countries. Some foreigners formed criminal organizations and began committing violent crimes, burglaries, and other offenses. The number of Penal Code arrests of foreign nationals visiting Japan was 867 cases in Showa 55 (1980), when statistics were first compiled, but by Heisei 6 (1994) it had increased approximately 15-fold to 13,321 cases. (3) Ensuring Community Safety and the Birth of Community Safety Policing

The crime situation from the Showa 60s through the Heisei era showed an increasingly pronounced deterioration, with the number of reported Penal Code offenses -- which had been on an upward trend since Showa 49 (1974) -- reaching approximately 1.8 million cases in Heisei 5 (1993). Against a backdrop of weakening community solidarity and increasing anonymity, it was pointed out that the crime-deterrence function inherent in local communities was declining, and public anxiety about crime also grew.

To strengthen community-based police activities, the police promoted the renewal and strengthening of community policing (Heisei 4 / 1992), advancing reforms to operations and activities such as koban block deployment and the placement of koban consultation officers, as well as infrastructure improvements. Starting in Heisei 5 (1993), efforts to prevent crime and other problems in communities (Community Safety Activities) were pursued more vigorously in cooperation with local residents and municipal governments, with proactive support for volunteers and outreach to local authorities.

Furthermore, building on this series of developments, in Heisei 6 (1994), a large-scale organizational restructuring -- the first in approximately 30 years -- was carried out. The Public Safety Division, which had been part of the Criminal Affairs Bureau of the NPA, was reorganized into the Community Safety Bureau, and a comprehensive approach to promoting measures for ensuring the safety and peace of citizens' daily lives was adopted.

社会の国際化が進むにつれ、近隣諸国との賃金格差を背景に不法就労事案が増加し、外国人の一部は犯罪組織を形成して凶悪犯罪や侵入盗等を敢行するようになった。来日外国人の刑法犯は、統計を開始した昭和55年には検挙件数が867件であったが、平成6年には1万3,321件と約15倍の増加を示した。他方、サミットや国際連合等でも国際組織犯罪対策が重要な政策課題として取り上げられ、我が国も国際社会への貢献が求められるようになった。

（3）地域の安全確保と生活安全警察の誕生

昭和60年代から平成にかけての犯罪情勢は、昭和49年から増加に転じた刑法犯認知件数が平成5年に約180万件となるなど、悪化傾向が一段と顕著になった。地域の連帯意識の希薄化や匿名性の増大等を背景として、地域社会に内在していた犯罪抑止機能の低下が指摘され、国民の犯罪に対する不安感も増大した。

警察は、地域に密着した警察活動を強化するため、地域警察の刷新強化（4年）を推進し、交番のブロック運用、交番相談員の配置等、地域警察の運用・活動の見直しや基盤整備を進めた。また、5年からは、地域において犯罪等を防止する活動（地域安全活動）を地域住民、自治体と連携してより強力に推進することとし、ボランティアに対する支援や自治体への働きかけを積極的に実施した。

さらに、こうした一連の動きを踏まえ、6年には、約30年振りに大規模な組織改編を行って、それまで警察庁刑事局に置かれていた保安部は生活安全局に改組され、市民生活の安全と平穏の確保に向けた施策を総合的に推進することとした。

(2) Advancement of Traffic Policing

Traffic fatalities numbered 10,679 in Heisei 7 (1995), the last year the figure exceeded 10,000. Every subsequent year fell below that threshold, and in Heisei 15 (2003) the toll dropped to 7,702 -- falling below 8,000 for the first time in 46 years since Showa 32 (1957). This is proof that the series of police measures has made a major contribution to ensuring traffic safety. Nevertheless, the situation remains severe: the number of accidents and injuries reached their worst levels ever. Moreover, the increase in traffic volume has caused congestion and pollution, degrading urban functionality and the quality of the living environment.

To improve this situation, the police adopted information and communications technology to upgrade traffic control systems, accurately managing networked traffic signals, electronic information boards, and other infrastructure to smooth traffic flow and prevent accidents. New systems were also put into practical use, such as priority signal control for buses and congestion information provided via car navigation devices, enabling many road users to benefit from improved convenience.

Meanwhile, against the backdrop of a declining birthrate and aging population, the police also devoted efforts to making road traffic barrier-free. In cooperation with road administrators, they promoted safety measures on residential streets and narrow roads in residential areas and city centers. They also expanded the introduction of pedestrian-vehicle separated signal control and advanced the installation of barrier-free signal systems to support safe mobility for the elderly, the visually impaired, and others.

(3) Police Reform Aimed at Restoring Public Trust

In September of Heisei 11 (1999), a series of scandals within the Kanagawa Prefectural Police was exposed through media reporting, drawing severe criticism from the public. In response, the NPA submitted a Police Law amendment bill to the Diet with provisions for strengthened inspections and other measures (the bill was subsequently abandoned). However, scandals continued to emerge -- including the disclosure in January of Heisei 12 (2000) of improper handling by the Niigata Prefectural Police of a girl's confinement case -- and criticism of the police intensified further. The National Public Safety Commission called for the establishment of a "Police Reform Council" composed of experts from various fields to restore public trust as quickly as possible. In July, the Council compiled its "Emergency Recommendations Regarding Police Reform." The National Public Safety Commission and the NPA took these recommendations very seriously, formulating the "Police Reform Outline" in August and submitting a new Police Law amendment bill to the Diet in September. The bill was passed with a majority vote of the three ruling parties and others, and was promulgated in December.

Following the enforcement of the amended law, the police have strengthened the self-correcting function of police administration through the operation of a written complaint filing system and the implementation of rigorous inspections. They have worked to establish "a police for the people" by establishing police station consultation councils and strengthening police safety consultations, while also periodically publishing reports on progress and seeking public evaluation to ensure the steady implementation of these measures.

As public safety conditions continued at the worst levels since the postwar period, criminal organizations such as yakuza (boryokudan), foreign criminal organizations operating in Japan, and drug and firearms smuggling rings were deepening their mutual cooperation, and their crimes were becoming ever more complex and sophisticated. Meanwhile, since the simultaneous terrorist attacks in the United States, the international terrorism situation had remained unpredictable, and the threat of international terrorism was becoming a reality even within Japan. Furthermore, cyber crimes such as fraud exploiting the internet were emerging as new social problems.

Because such crimes had the potential to seriously impact national public safety and could easily cross national and prefectural borders, it became necessary to examine what security responsibilities the national government should fulfill and how the roles of national and local government should be divided within the framework of the current police system, in which prefectural police bear full responsibility for policing activities within their respective jurisdictions.

To address these new security threats, in 2004 (Heisei 16) an amendment to the Police Act was enacted with two pillars: reorganization of the National Police Agency and clarification of the national government's responsibility for public security. It came into effect in April of the same year.

The Anti-Boryokudan Division was reorganized into the Organized Crime Division, enabling the integrated promotion of anti-boryokudan measures, drug and firearms countermeasures, and countermeasures against crimes by foreign nationals visiting Japan -- functions that had previously been handled by separate divisions. At the same time, the national government's involvement was strengthened regarding liaison with foreign police administrative bodies such as the International Criminal Police Organization (ICPO/Interpol).

A new Foreign Affairs Intelligence Division was established within the Security Bureau to enhance intelligence-gathering and analytical capabilities regarding terrorism and espionage activities. At the same time, the national government's involvement was strengthened in responding to major terrorist incidents such as bomb attacks and NBC terrorism (terrorism using nuclear, biological, or chemical materials), as well as terrorist incidents overseas in which Japanese nationals become victims.

The national government assumed overall control of technical support for cyber crime investigations, with the national information and communications division taking charge of analytical operations at prefectural police investigation sites. Additionally, a new Information Technology Crime Countermeasures Division was established within the Community Safety Bureau to strengthen coordination functions related to cyber crime investigations.

In the past, Japan's public safety was generally regarded as being maintained at a good level, but in recent years the situation has been deteriorating steadily. The sharp increase in residential burglaries and street crimes has made citizens acutely aware that criminal victimization has become a close-to-home reality, while the frequent occurrence of violent juvenile crimes has generated momentum for a reassessment of the social conditions that bred them. The increasing violence and organization of crimes by foreign nationals and their spread across the nation have made it clear that the negative aspects of internationalization are taking deep root in Japanese society.

On the other hand, it has become evident that the justice and administrative systems confronting crime as a whole lack capacity and are declining in function. The workload on frontline police has reached a serious level, compounded by the increasing precision required in criminal justice proceedings and the growth in consultation services, and the clearance rate for Penal Code offenses has fallen to approximately 20% -- the lowest postwar level. In this way, the current situation is truly critical, and now that the current police system has reached its 50th anniversary, the true worth of the police is once again being called into question.

This special feature has reviewed the postwar Japanese police decade by decade, and finally, as guidelines for the direction Japanese police should take over the next ten years, we introduce the initiatives undertaken by the Crime Countermeasures Ministerial Meeting. Whether the coming decade will be looked back upon as a "lost decade" or praised as an "era of V-shaped recovery" depends entirely on the efforts of the police, and the responsibility is heavy.

From September 2003 (Heisei 15), Prime Minister Koizumi convened the "Crime Countermeasures Ministerial Meeting," composed of all cabinet ministers, with the aim of restoring Japan's status as "the world's safest country." The "Three Perspectives for Security Recovery" presented at this meeting serve not only as vantage points for planning, implementing, and evaluating individual measures, but also function as guiding principles for realizing comprehensive and all-encompassing crime countermeasures.

① Support for citizens' activities to ensure their own safety

Good public safety is not maintained solely through police patrols and crime enforcement; each individual citizen is called upon to promote voluntary initiatives to secure safe living in their communities. It is important to channel residents' desire to "do something for safety" into concrete action, and the national government must support residents' voluntary efforts through providing information, promoting understanding of crime prevention equipment, and similar measures.

② Development of social environments resistant to crime

It is important not only to enforce the law against crime, but also to promote measures recognized as directly or indirectly effective in deterring crime, thereby building social environments in which crime is less likely to occur. This includes restoring community solidarity and family bonds, reviving functions that deter crime and juvenile delinquency, incorporating crime prevention perspectives into the design of roads, parks, buildings, and other structures, and examining policies for accepting foreign nationals that take into account the impact on public safety -- all to be promoted from every angle in a multifaceted manner.

③ Various crime countermeasures including border control measures

To respond effectively to crimes that are becoming increasingly international and sophisticated, greater cooperation transcending "ministry walls" and more effective use of information are required, and it is necessary to consider frameworks to that end. Border control measures in particular are a field where cross-cutting national initiatives are especially expected, since accurate immigration control is indispensable and the success or failure of such measures hinges on cooperation among relevant ministries and liaison with foreign agencies.

1　今後10年間の日本の警察

かつて、我が国の治安は総じて良好に保たれていると評価されていたが、近年、その水準は悪化の一途をたどっている。侵入犯罪・街頭犯罪の急増は、犯罪被害が身近になったことを国民に実感させ、凶悪な少年犯罪の多発は、その土壌となった社会風潮の見直し気運を生じさせている。外国人犯罪の凶悪化・組織化と全国への拡散は、国際化の負の側面が深刻な形で我が国社会に根付きつつあることを知らしめた。

他方、犯罪に対峙する司法・行政システム全般の容量不足と機能低下が明らかになってきた。第一線警察の業務負担は、刑事司法の精密化や相談業務の増加等とも相まって深刻な状況にあり、刑法犯検挙率は約2割と戦後最低の水準に落ち込んでいる。このように、現下の状況は正に危機的であり、現行警察制度が施行されて50年を迎えた今、警察の真価が改めて問われている。

本特集では、戦後の日本警察を10年ごとに回顧してきたが、最後に、今後10年間に日本警察が進むべき指針として、犯罪対策閣僚会議による取組みを紹介する。将来、この10年間が「失われた10年」と顧みられるか、「V字回復の時代」と評されるかは、警察の取組み如何にかかっており、その責任は重い。

（1）治安回復のための3つの視点

平成15年9月から、小泉内閣総理大臣は、「世界一安全な国、日本」の復活を目指し、全閣僚を構成員とする「犯罪対策閣僚会議」を開催している。この会議で示された「治安回復のための3つの視点」は、個別の施策を立案・実施・評価するための視座を提供するだけでなく、総合的で包括的な犯罪対策を実現するための理念としても機能している。

Box: 治安回復のための3つの視点 Three Perspectives for Security Recovery

① 国民が自らの安全を確保するための活動の支援

良好な治安は、警察のパトロールや犯罪の取締りのみによって保たれるものではなく、国民一人一人が地域において安全な生活の確保のための自発的な取組みを推進することが求められている。「安全確保のために何かしたい」という住民の思いを具体的な行動に昇華させていくことが重要であり、国は、情報の提供や防犯設備への理解の普及等を通じ、住民の自主的な取組みを支援していくことが必要である。

② 犯罪の生じにくい社会環境の整備

犯罪の取締りのみならず、犯罪の抑止に直接・間接に有効であると認められる取組みを推進し、犯罪の生じにくい社会環境を整備することが重要である。地域の連帯や家族の絆を取り戻し、犯罪や少年非行を抑止する機能を再生すること、道路、公園、建物等の設計に防犯の視点を織り込むこと、治安に及ぼす影響を踏まえた外国人受入れ方策を検討することなど、あらゆる観点から多角的に推進することが重要である。

③ 水際対策を始めとした各種犯罪対策

国際化・高度化する犯罪に的確に対応していくためには、従前以上に「省庁の壁」を超えた一層の連携と情報の有効活用が求められており、そのための枠組みの検討も視野に入れる必要がある。特に、水際対策は、的確な出入国管理が不可欠な上、その成否は関係省庁間の協働及び外国機関との連携が鍵を握っているため、国による横断的な取組みが特に期待される分野である。

Based on these three perspectives, the Crime Countermeasures Ministerial Meeting formulated, in December 2003 (Heisei 15), the "Action Plan for the Realization of a Society Resistant to Crime," with the goal of eliminating public anxiety about security, halting the rise in crime, and escaping the critical security situation within approximately five years. This action plan established five priority issues corresponding to the characteristic trends of the current crime situation, and it was determined that each measure would be steadily implemented with the cooperation of citizens, businesses, local governments, and other stakeholders.

1. Deterrence of everyday crimes that threaten peaceful living - Revitalization of community solidarity and realization of safe and secure community-building - Promotion and spread of products, systems, etc. effective in crime prevention - Protection of crime victims

2. Deterrence of juvenile crime through society-wide efforts - Strict and appropriate response to juvenile crime - Efforts toward healthy upbringing that contribute to prevention of juvenile delinquency - Strengthening of cooperation among relevant agencies to protect juveniles from delinquency

3. Response to threats that cross borders - Promotion of surveillance and enforcement at borders - Promotion of countermeasures against illegal entry and illegal overstay - Strengthening of investigations into crimes by foreign nationals visiting Japan - Strengthening of cooperation with foreign-related agencies

4. Protection of the economy and society from organized crime and similar threats - Promotion of organized crime countermeasures and anti-boryokudan measures - Realization of a society free from drug abuse and firearms crime - Promotion of countermeasures against various offenses committed in an organized manner - Promotion of cyber crime countermeasures

5. Development of the foundation for security recovery

After 50 years, the police system has matured, but new security threats continue to emerge without pause, and the police must continue to respond accurately to changes in society going forward. Moreover, as indicated in the action plan, the recovery of public safety is not achieved through crime enforcement alone, but through administrative agencies working as one while gaining the cooperation of citizens and advancing multifaceted initiatives. The police are called upon not only to fulfill their own duties, but also to create an environment in which all sectors of society can participate in efforts toward security recovery, and to play the role of harmonizing those efforts.

Concluding Paragraph

50年の時を経て警察制度は成熟したが、新たな治安事象は絶え間なく出現しており、今後も警察は常に社会の変化に的確に対応していく必要がある。また、行動計画に示されたとおり、治安の回復は、犯罪の取締りのみの力によるのではなく、国民の協力を得ながら行政機関が一体となり、多角的な取組みを進めることで果たされるものである。警察は、自らの職責を果たすことはもちろんのこと、社会各層が治安回復に向けた取組みに参画するための環境整備を行うとともに、それらの調和を図る役割を担うことが求められている。
